Spider-Man, McFarlane, and the Impact of "Torment"

About Spider-man

Spider-Man is a web-crawling crime fighter who has fought countless villains in his effdorts to save the innocent citizens of New York City. Created in 1962 by Stan Lee and Steven Ditko, Spider-Man first appeared in Amazing Fantasy #15 during the Silver Age of Comic Books and was well-received among the general public, sparking his own line of comics titled The Amazing Spider-Man. The Amazing Spider-Man was the longest running series of Spider-Man comics under Marvel and featured some of the most noteable scenes and villains in Spider-Man's career. Spider-Man has been featured in other series under Marvel including The Spectacular Spider-Man, Web of Spider-Man and much more.

The mask of Spider-Man hides the identity of Peter Parker, a teenager living in New York City who was bitten by a radioactive spider. The bite gives Peter superhuman abilities, including increased strength, the ability to climb walls and buildings, and the ability to shoot webs. Peter uses these new powers combined with his wit to fight crime and any threats against him, his family and friends, and the people of New York City. Throughout his battles in the comics, storylines arise that challenge Peter to make decisions that affect the fate of others, sometimes presenting a problem much greater than battling any villain that stands in his way.

McFarlane's decision to start his own Spider-Man series -- simply titled Spider-Man -- was unprecedented given his prior experience creating art for Amazing Spider-Man #298 - #328. After being dissatisfied with the inability to control the storylines of the series, McFarlane broke off and wrote the new Spider-Man collection under Marvel Comics in 1990. McFarlane's Spider-Man collection began with the five-part "Torment" story arch, continuing with 11 more issues (although McFarlane only did covers for 15 comics total) until Spider-Man #16

The Torment Series

"Torment" is the beginning of McFarlane's individual Spider-Man project. With the release of Spider-Man #1 in August 1990, about 2.5 million copies were sold due in large part to the variant covers released by Marvel to encourage people to collect all three covers. Although the series was another display of McFarlane's distinct style, "Torment" was the first example of McFarlane's storyline with Spider-Man where he had close-to full control over the comic. McFarlane decided to feature Spider-Man and infamous foe "The Lizard" for his opening story arch, with a few twists sprinkled throughout the series. The five-part series set the stage for a unique collection of storylines that included vivid frames, crossover of other Marvel characters, and brand new storylines.
